Our research program aims to understand the effects of different types of hearing loss on neural and perceptual responses to sound, and to translate this knowledge to improving diagnostics and rehabilitative strategies to improve the lives of people with hearing loss.  We take a multi-disciplinary approach by coordinating the use of single-unit neurophysiology, non-invasive electrophysiology, behavior, anatomy/histology, and computational modeling to provide quantitative links between physiology, anatomy, and perception.  To maximize translational relevance, we collaborate with the SNAPlab and MD Steer Audiology clinic at Purdue through coordinated animal and human studies that synergistically bridge the gap between our knowledge of the physiological and perceptual effects of sensorineural hearing loss. We currently have projects addressing both basic-science (e.g., neural coding and behavioral studies) and translational questions (development of pre-clinical animal models of SNHL and audiology diagnostics).
